What to Observe


Participant Observation
  • Observe people doing their work and living their lives - gets beyond things that people say are wrong or broken e.g. living in another country, plenty of things are normal to local but unfamiliar to outside.
  • Time-consuming, difficulty documenting data, subjective, Hawthorne effect (people behave differently when observed), context may make it intrusive.

Diary Studies
  • Effective (longitudinal) method where direct observation would be overly intrusive.
  • Participants self report - write notes themselves, may be given specific time and specific things to write about.
  • However, you may be changing their behaviour by making them write notes.

ESM/EMA
  • Experience Sampling Method / Ecological Momentary Assessment.
  • Participants promoted to record some information during daily lives e.g. emotion.
  • Two methods: prompt triggered by regular time interval or contextual event.

Interviewing
  • Extract specific and intimate knowledge and emotions from target use group.
  • Questions can be informed from participant observation or evidence from logged files.
  • Structured: Scripted questions - more scope for consistency across interviews.
  • Semi-structured: Use of script but interviewer can ask follow-up questions.
  • Unstructured: Interviewer can ask anything within reason and context.


Good Need-finding tools
  • Good environments - friendly and safe for all participants.
  • Structure and flexibility - have plan and goals but pursue new opportunities.
  • Methods to avoid Hawthorne effect - distortion of behaviour due to observation.
  • Room for silence, giving participants time to speak.

Communicating Requirements
  • Distil insights gained through need-finding, aim to communicate back to user group and other members of design team.
  • Functional requirements: What system must do.
  • Non-functional requirements: Properties of system e.g. performance, security.
  • Can be represented in many forms including the following:

Personas
Abstract user who represents what you have found during need-finding. Includes demographic information, motivations, beliefs, intentions behaviours and goals. User profile type of thing.
Scenarios
Stories about people undertaking activities, can feature one or more developed personas. Can describe how issues can be overcome using design or technology that is being developed.

Scenario Example
Jeff gets home from work late, exhausted. He is aware that he has to cook for his family quickly as it is already around the meal time. Being a meticulous forward planner, Jeff returns home with a plan in his mind as to what is in the fridge, but to his horror, upon opening the fridge to select out his ingredients, he realises that a few key items have been eaten already by his family, so he needs a new plan and quickly. This is where the fridge prompts him with a recipe using items in the house, and offers the ability to reorder items so to restock ahead of the next mealtime. Being a detail oriented person, Jeff is aware of the dietary requirements of his children and is keen that the fridge does not simply replenish unhealthy items with similarly unhealthy items, but offers healthy alternatives and allows him input as to what is available to his children in the house.
